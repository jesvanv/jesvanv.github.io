Teaching Lesson 1.3

Students should complete Chapter 1 Pretest and Chapter 1 Study Guide before starting the lesson cycle.

The Study Guide prepares students for chapter content by summarizing main points and vocabulary. Student should read the Study Guide aloud.

Engage

Class Time: 15 minutes for discussion

Connect to Prior Knowledge: What makes an investigation?

Ask students to think about how they investigate and collect data in their daily lives. Is setting up scientific or laboratory equipment always involved? What are some alternative ways to conduct investigations? Have students share an experience about a time when they were curious or puzzled about something. Which problem-solving techniques did they apply in order to arrive at a solution?

VOCABULARY

system – a group of objects and the factors that affect the objects

variable – a factor that affects how a system works

descriptive investigation – used for exploring and describing new things

experimental investigation – uses a controlled experiment to look for relationships between variables

manipulated variable or independent variable – a variable that is changed in an experiment

responding variable or dependent variable – a variable that is affected by a change in the manipulated variable

control variable – a variable that is held constant in an experiment

graph – a picture that shows how two variables are related

The word variable is from the Latin varius which means “different.” Ask students to reflect on this meaning as they try to create a list of words which share its origin. Some examples may include variety or various. Use these words in sentences to help students understand that vari- means different.

There are various science courses offered at our school.

The gardener was happy to see the variety of plants available at the local nursery.

Systems

Write the word system on the board. Ask students, “What is a system?” List students’ answers on the board. Divide students into small groups of three to five. Assign each team one system from the list they generated. Distribute chart paper to each group, and have students identify the components of their system. Finally, instruct students to create a visual which shows how all the members of the system work together and are affected by one another. Have students present their systems to the class.

If students experience difficulty generating a list of systems, then prompt them with a few examples. Some possible answers include an aquarium, the public school system, a computer, an orchestra, the human digestive system, or the solar system.

Explore

Class Time: 40 minutes

Students will identify the variables that affect the speed of a car rolling down a ramp, design an experiment where there are several variables to be controlled, and recognize a poorly designed experiment where more than one variable has been changed.

Setup Instructions

Materials
  1. Students work in groups of 3-5
  2. Place different amounts of weights on several cars so there are cars with 1 weight, 2 weights, 3 weights, and no weights being used by different groups in the class.
  3. Print answer sheets or write notes & answers in notebooks.

Car and ramp

CPO DataCollector and photogates

Physics stand

Small weight set

Investigation 1.3 Systems, Experiments, and Variables

Excellent learning opportunities present themselves to your students when you randomize the variables that affect the speed of the car in this investigation. Students will discover your “tricks” and it will teach them to create good experiments. Before class, add some weights to some cars so that there are cars with three weights, two weights, one weight, and no weights. Check that the wheels of all the cars have been sprayed with a spray lubricant. The wheels should spin freely. Cars with sticky wheels will not produce accurate results.

1. Setting up the experiment

Teaching with wrong answers

We usually learn more from our mistakes than from our successes. Students learn problem-solving skills by actually solving problems, not by reading about how to solve problems. In the quest for the “teachable moment,” it is very useful to set up things so that they don’t work right. If the students can figure out how to fix things, they learn far more effectively than if everything always goes like a movie script. The powerful part of this Investigation is when the students figure out why the experiment did not “work.” When they fix the variables, everything works perfectly. This is a really useful skill. The next time a student confronts a situation that is perplexing, they might remember to think about what other variables might be acting besides the ones that are obvious.

Of course, it is always a good idea if you, the teacher, know how it works and what the important variables are so you can moderate student frustration!

We are going to use the DataCollector and photogates to measure how fast the car goes down the ramp. I would like each group to put the ramp on the pole in a different hole. Counting from the bottom, put your ramp in hole number 5, yours in hole number 7, yours in hole number 9, etc.

Each group should set their ramp at a different hole in the pole. Separate the ramps by two holes for the best results. For example, set groups at holes #3, #5, #7, #9, and #11.

Put your two photogates on the ramp; first the A photogate and then the B photogate. You can put them anywhere you wish. Make sure the light beams are unblocked (nothing is in the way of the light beam) when the car is at the starting place.

Which ramp will have the fastest car?

You are setting up an experiment to test the obvious hypothesis that the steeper ramp should have the fastest car. Students will know intuitively that the car on the steeper ramp must go faster, and the confirmation will give students confidence that they can understand the numbers.

But, first we want to teach the importance of variables, so we want to create a situation where the numbers do not confirm what we think should happen. We will then fix the experimental variables and show that if we are more careful, everything really does work. Discreetly make sure some ramps have the photogates close and some far apart. Allow some students to push the car down the ramp.

Should the fastest car have the longest time or the shortest time to get from A to B?

The students should recognize that the fastest car would have the shortest time.

I want everyone to drop the car down once, and read me what time you get from photogate A to photogate B. You can drop the car any way you want.

Make a table on the board like the sketch shown here. Have each group drop the car and measure the time it takes to get from photogate A to photogate B. Write the times down in the table.

Write the times on the board. Without controlling the variables, they should not be in the order you expect.

 Because the variables are scrambled, the results will not come out with the shortest time being the steepest ramp, second shortest, second steepest, etc.

Do these numbers make sense to you? You thought the steepest ramp should have the fastest car. Why doesn’t this ramp have a time that is shorter than this one (point to two that are out of sequence)?

Why didn’t it work?

The class should recognize that the distance between the photogates should be the same for the experiment to be fair. Also they should start to question other variables, such as pushing, weight, etc.

2. Variables in an experiment

We are testing to see if changing the height of the ramp makes the car faster. What do we need to do to make the experiment a fair test?

We need to control the variables so all are the same except for the one we are testing (height). The students discovered the variables and now have a first-hand experience of their importance. Discuss how you can’t tell if the changes in time from different ramps are from the different angles, or pushing the car differently, or having different spacing/position of the photogates.

Variables are the things that affect experiments and can cause changes in the experiment’s results. What are the variables that affect the time we measured?

Prompt the students to identify as many variables as they can think of. Make a list of them on the board as the students call them out. It may take hinting to get some of them.

3. Doing a controlled experiment

We are going to do a more scientific test of speed based on the angle of the ramp for our second experiment. This means we have to control the variables more carefully. Use the ruler, and put the first photogate exactly 1 foot (12 inches) from the bottom of the ramp. Put the second photogate 1 foot higher than the first. Make your measurements from the bottom of the photogate to the bottom edge of the photogate.

Circulate and check that the measurements are being completed correctly. Students should always measure from the bottom edge of the photogate.

What shall we do with the other variables?

Everyone should agree that the other variables should be the same.

Why don’t we agree to start at the top, not push the car, and have one weight on the car?


Doing a controlled experiment

A good technique for dropping the car is to hold one finger on the top of the screw and gently releasing it when the car is all as high up as it will go.

Students should be able to get three times that are within 0.0005 seconds of each other.

The distance between the photogates is 1 foot.

The position of the first photogate is 1 foot from the bottom of the ramp.

The height of the ramp is different since this is the variable we are testing.

Friction is the same for all cars (an assumption).

Try to perfect a drop technique that gives no push at the top. This is tricky. You know you have it when you can get three measurements that are within 0.001 seconds of each other or better.

We have regularly seen careful students do three trials that agree to within plus or minus 0.0002 seconds. The objective is to set a challenge (make three identical times) that also teaches the students to develop a technique that will yield consistent results. This gradual progression of skills is essential so that when students start making graphs and predictions, their data collection technique has progressed to the point where the numbers are good enough to make usable graphs.

Roll your cars down again, and tell me the times you get now that we have carefully controlled the variables.

Have students roll the cars again, and call out their times. Write the times on the board in similar columns as before. They should all be in the proper order with the steepest ramp having the shortest time, the second steepest ramp having the second shortest time, and so on. The shallowest ramp has the longest time. All the times should correspond to what everyone thought would happen.

The power of collective thinking

When an entire class becomes engaged in an activity, the whole is far more than the sum of the parts in terms of how much learning happens. Stimulate thinking by continually asking questions, withholding answers if the students are unable to answer right away, and giving tantalizing hints. You are there to help them learn to think for themselves. Dialog sharpens students’ critical communication skills as well as teaches them your subject!

4. Applying what you have learned

Science is about repeatability, reproducibility, and drawing conclusions from accurate evidence. These results show us that the measurements confirm the car on the steepest ramp goes fastest. Why didn’t the first experiment show the same thing? Why were the times not in the order we expected?

Explain and discuss the words repeatability, reproducibility, and drawing conclusions. Probe the class until they say something about the variables. They should recognize that they were more careful with variables in the second experiment.

The variables were not controlled in our first height experiment. In the second experiment, we had a more scientific test because we only changed one variable at a time. If we wanted to see how the angle of the ramp affected the speed, we needed to be sure all the other variables were the same. Otherwise, we could not tell if changes in the time were caused by the change in height, the way we measured time, the way we pushed the car, or its weight

Investigation 1.3: Data and Answers

1. Setting up the experiment

  1. The shortest time should be the ramp with the steepest angle.
  2. Students should write their own time from A to B. Typical numbers are between 0.05 seconds and 0.75 seconds.
  3. The times should have gone in order with the steepest ramp having the shortest time. They did not go in order because many things besides the ramp angle were changed.
  4. A better test would be to set everyone’s photogates in the exact same place, give everyone the same number of weights, and keep everything the same except the angles of the ramps.

2. Variables in an experiment

Variables that affect the speed of the car:

  1. Distance between photogates.
  2. Position of photogates (top, middle, bottom of ramp).
  3. Weight of the car.
  4. Starting point on the ramp.
  5. Push or no push at start (start technique).
  6. Angle of the ramp (test variable).
  7. Friction.

3. Doing a controlled experiment

  1. The times agreed with our hypothesis. The steepest ramp had the shortest time. The second steepest ramp had the second shortest time, and so on.
  2. This experiment was better because we changed only one variable (angle) and kept all the others constant. That way we were sure that the variable we changed is what caused the differences in times. The experiment clearly showed a relationship between the ramp angle and the time it took the car to get from photogate A to photogate B.

4. Applying what you learned

  1. The steel hits first because there is less air friction than on the paper, not because the steel is heavier and heavier objects fall faster.
  2. You might test the effect of weight on speed by keeping the ramp angle and all other variables constant.

Explain

Class Time: 40 minutes

Assign the Lesson 1.3 Reading: Systems, Experiments, and Models. Use the Lesson 1.3 Presentation Slides as a guide to presenting and discussing the content in the reading.

Guided Discussion: Types of Investigations

Lead a discussion to help students distinguish among different types of investigations. Ask students to plan each type of investigation, record a plan in their notebook, and discuss their plan.

Descriptive investigations

These investigations involve collecting data which is used to reach conclusions about systems. Although it focuses on a research question and has a method (or process), a descriptive investigation has no hypothesis. The investigator simply records observations without making any comparisons or manipulating variables. For example, a student may examine a rock formation to observe its features or to identify distinct layers.

Comparative investigations

Emphasize the word compare as you discuss this type of investigation. Comparative investigations focus on collecting data about different objects, organisms, events, or under different conditions and then using the data to making comparisons. These investigations have a research question, documented method, and hypothesis. But, comparative investigations are not controlled. For example, a student may compare and contrast the reactivity of several rock samples when each is placed in a hydrochloric acid solution.

Experimental investigations

These are the controlled experiments which are designed to change only one variable at a time so that a causal relationship can be determined. The data collected in experimental investigations serve as evidence of a relationship between variables. For example, a student may test the relationship between the launch angle of a projectile the distance it travels. All variables of the system remain constant except the launch angle.

Present the following research questions to students and have them tell which of the three types of investigation is most appropriate to arrive at an answer.

  1. Where do deer go when their habitat is disturbed by the construction of a new housing development?
  2. Is there a difference in the cases of respiratory illnesses among human populations in Harris County during the summer months as compared to the winter months?
  3. How does water temperature affect the number of eggs produced by largemouth bass?

Then use one example investigation to illustrate how a research question may be altered to fit each type. As stated, Example 3 (above) is an experimental investigation. If changed to “Do largemouth bass produce more eggs in October or March?” then it becomes comparative. A descriptive investigation may question “How do largemouth bass protect their eggs?”

Elaborate

Class Time: 50 minutes

Exploring models

Addressing Misconceptions
about Science and Technology

Students often think of science and technology as synonyms. Although there is a relationship between science and technology, they are not the same. Ask your students if they think it is correct to use the words science and technology interchangeably. Then have them explain why they feel as they do. It is probable that your students will initially disagree.

How do your students define science? Solicit ideas and then have a volunteer read aloud the definition(s) from a dictionary. Then have your students compare this with the description of technology from the lesson reading. Students should infer that science and technology are indeed different.

Read aloud each statement below and have students classify it as a description of either science or technology.

The president of the company was excited about the new manufacturing methods which led to increased production. (technology)

The teacher displayed pictures of the types of plant life commonly found in tropical rainforests. (science)

Joseph explained that gravity is responsible for the object falling to Earth. (science)

The new building is in an area prone to hurricanes. Therefore, it was constructed to withstand winds of up to 150 miles per hour. (technology)

A steel nail placed in water will rust. (science)

When thinking about models, students may be tempted to focus exclusively on physical models, such as bridges or airplanes. This is a great place to start, but the discussion can and should expand much farther. Ask your students these questions.

  • What is a model?
  • Why are models important?
  • Are all models physical?
  • What are some examples of models?

Steer students toward understanding that models help us to understand how systems work. Have students build paper airplanes. Before building the airplanes, ask students to visualize how to design an airplane that will fly farther than any other classmate’s airplane. Use this time to talk about conceptual and mental models, variables that may affect the airplanes’ performance, and experimental design.

Allow students to design, construct, and test their airplanes. Tell students to record their observations. Explain how scientists use mathematical models, such as graphs, to describe their data and observations. Encourage students to discuss ways to improve the design and performance of the airplane; and then retest to find out if their modifications worked.
