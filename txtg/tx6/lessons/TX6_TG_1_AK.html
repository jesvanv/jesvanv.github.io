Reading 1.3

  6. Book problems usually have only one correct answer. Design problems are more interesting because they can be solved more than one way.

Chapter 1 Connection

  1. Scientists study volcanoes on Earth to help predict eruptions more accurately. Many people throughout the world live near volcanoes and safety is an issue. Volcanic activity also impacts climate. Climate changes can affect people, animals, plants, and natural resources. Scientists hope to learn more about volcanoes by studying them on other planets. Io and Titan are two planetary moons with volcanic activity. Many planets have volcanoes that provide valuable information about how a planet and even the solar system have evolved. Understanding how volcanoes behave in our and other atmospheres is very important to scientists.
  2. On Earth, scientists use satellites and aircraft to study volcanoes. People can study volcanoes by actually travelling to them and viewing them like Dr. Lopes did at Mt. Etna. However, volcanoes in space are much more difficult to see. For these volcanoes, scientists use spacecraft, satellite, radar images, spectrometers, spectroscopes, and telescopes to gather data. NASA space missions like the Galileo and Cassini missions help scientists gather information about volcanoes on other planets.
  3. Dr. Lopes communicates her findings in a variety of ways. She writes papers and reports that are published in scientific journals. She gives many talks to other scientists and to the public. Dr. Lopes runs seminars at universities, astronomy clubs, and at her own place of work at NASAs Jet Propulsion Lab in California. In addition, Dr. Lopes has appeared on television programs, been part of documentaries, and written a book.
  4. Dr. Lopes roles include planetary volcanologist, author, world-record holder, and mother.

Chapter 1 Activity

Safety note: Demonstrator should wear eye protection throughout this activity. Conduct the activity in a well-ventilated area. This demonstration uses isopropyl alcohol, an eye irritant. Isopropyl alcohol is harmful if ingested or if concentrated fumes are inhaled. Be sure to dispose of the alcohol immediately following the demonstration. Isopropyl alcohol can be washed down a sink with plenty of water.

Teacher Notes: During this activity, Cup A is filled with isopropyl (rubbing) alcohol and Cup B is filled with water. When the students first observe each cup, they appear to be identical (color, consistency, etc.). Then you go through different tests to show a clear difference in their chemical properties. Be vague with directions about observations so that students will think carefully about what an observation is (versus an opinion or inference).

Test 1: Pour a small amount of each liquid into the appropriate cup (enough so that an ice cube can be suspended in each cup). Put one ice cube in each of the cups. Say to students, Write down what you are observing.

Cup A: ice cub sinks; Cup B: ice cube floats.

Test 2: Using an eye dropper, take a drop of each liquid and squirt it onto the surface of the counter. Say to students, Write down what you are observing.

Cup A: Drop of liquid spreads out and seems to evaporate; Cup B: Drop of liquid beads up.

Test 3: Using another eye dropper, take a drop of each liquid and drop it onto a piece of paper. Walk around the room so all students can see and say to students Write down what you are observing.

Cup A: spreads out and saturates the paper; Cup B: beads up.

At the conclusion of the tests, have students raise their hands and share observations. Undoubtedly, you will have some students who share opinions or inferences. Write down what every student shares. However, put inferences in a separate column from observations. Do not tell students how you are organizing the data. At the conclusion of the data sharing ask the class, Why have I separated your thoughts into two columns? Have them discuss in groups. Have a class discussion and send the students to work on their questions.

    1. I used my sense of sight and smell. I used my sense of sight the most.
    2. I was good at making observations. I paid very close attention to what was going on. However, since the teacher was doing the demonstrations, it was harder to know exactly what was happening. I would have been able to make better observations if I had done the demonstration myself.
    3. An observation is a statement about something you see without any conclusions drawn. An inference is a conclusion or statement based on observations and prior experiences.
    4. I infer that the liquids are different based on the tests.
    5. No, the liquids were different. Based on my observations, I infer that the liquid in Cup A was rubbing alcohol and the liquid in Cup B was water.

Reading 1.3

  1. Question: Do birds prefer thistle seed or sunflower seed?
    Hypothesis: If given equal amounts of thistle seed or sunflower seed, birds will eat more of the sunflower seed.
    Procedure: Fill one feeder with 3 cups of thistle seed. Fill an identical feeder with 2 cups of sunflower seed. Place the feeders right next to each other and at the same height. Measure the amount of seed remaining in each feeder every 24 hours for a week.
  2. Answers are:
    1. If the drop height of the marble is decreased, then the indentation in the dough will be deeper.
    2. The manipulated variable is the drop height.
    3. The responding variable is the depth of the indentation.
    4. The marble used and the type/thickness/softness of the clay are control variables.
    5. Data includes the drop height and the depth of the indentation in the clay.
    6. Form the clay into a 3 cm cube. The top should be smooth. Drop the marble from a height of 30 cm onto the clay. Carefully remove the marble and measure the depth of the indentation in the clay. Repeat for 25, 20, 15, 10, and 5 cm drop heights. Use a fresh cube of clay each time.
    7. The data will probably disprove her hypothesis. If the marble is dropped from a greater height, it will be moving faster when it hits the clay.
