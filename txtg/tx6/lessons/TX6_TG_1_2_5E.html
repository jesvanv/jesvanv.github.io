Teaching Lesson 1.2

Students should complete Chapter 1 Pretest and Chapter 1 Study Guide before starting the lesson cycle.

The Study Guide prepares students for chapter content by summarizing main points and vocabulary. Student should read the Study Guide aloud.

Engage

Class Time: 20 minutes for discussion

Observations Lead to Questions

Briefly revisit the discussion about observations and inferences from Lesson 1.1. Discuss how humans spend quite a bit of time observing their surroundings. Then ask students to consider what they do when an observation leads to additional questions. Students may need prompting. For example, students may observe that leaves on a maple tree change color with the seasons and question why this happens. Encourage students to share other examples.

Describe how making observations and asking questions are important steps in the scientific process. Once a question has arisen, the next step is to offer an explanation or answer to the question raised. Tell students this tentative explanation is called a hypothesis. Then share this scenario with students:

A flashlight is placed in each room in your home. You have the responsibility of checking each flashlight on a monthly basis to ensure that it is working properly. You discover that the flashlight in your sister’s bedroom is not working.

Have students answer these questions based on the scenario.

  1. What are some questions you may ask as you try to figure out why the flashlight is not working? (Possible answers include: Is the switch turned to the “on” position? Are the batteries correctly positioned? Is the bulb intact?)
  2. Suppose you observe that the batteries are correctly positioned and are the proper size. However, your sister tells you that her friend Liz had been playing with the flashlight during her slumber party last weekend. The bulb is not broken but the flashlight still does not work when the switch is in the “on” position. Write a hypothesis that explains why you think the flashlight is not working.

VOCABULARY

hypothesis – a possible answer to a scientific question based on observations

data – pieces of information collected to test a hypothesis

scientific method – a series of steps including observing, forming a question, stating a hypothesis, collecting data, and reaching a conclusion

scientific law – a statement that describes an observed phenomenon; it is supported by evidence collected from many observations and experiments

scientific theory – a statement that explains a complex idea; it is supported by evidence collected from many experiments

Explore

Class Time: 50 minutes

Students will use their senses, apply deductive reasoning skills to solve problems, and apply steps of the scientific method to figure out the shape of an object inside a box without being able to look inside the box and actually see the object.

Setup Instructions

Materials
  1. Students work in groups of 3-5.
  2. Prepare boxes containing clay shapes prior to class time. You may wish to have students prepare the shapes the day before the investigation.
  3. Print answer sheets or write notes & answers in notebooks.

Small cardboard box containing a clay shape (sphere, cube, cylinder, or cone)

Investigation 1.2: Inquiry and Scientific Evidence

Doing scientific experiments is often like figuring the answer to a puzzle. Scientists must make observations that they then use to draw conclusions about the world around us. For example, suppose I walked into the classroom wearing a raincoat and dripping with water. What are the observations you would make?

The observations are seeing the raincoat and the water dripping off of it.

A hypothesis is a possible explanation based on your observations. What hypothesis would you make based on these observations?

Students may say that it is raining outside and that the teacher was outside in the rain wearing the raincoat. Students may also realize that there are other possibilities, such as a bucket of water or a sprinkler may have made the teacher’s coat wet.

In today’s investigation you will be making observations and trying to figure out what clay shape is inside of a box.

1. Setting up

Your box contains a sphere, cube, cylinder, or cone. It was made from a ball of clay approximately the size of a ping pong ball. You will have to use your observations to figure out which of these shapes is inside.

Give one box to each group.

2. Stop and think

Write down at one thing you know about your clay object by simply looking at your box.

Students should realize that they know the dimensions of the object are smaller than the dimensions of the box.

Think about what you could do to figure out which shape is in your box. Write down at least 3 observations you could make. Assume you can hold the box and do anything other than open it or damage it in any way.

Give students time to think and write.

3. Conducting your inquiry

What comes to mind when you hear the word, inquiry?

Students may or may not be familiar with the meaning of the term.

An inquiry is conducted to find the answers to questions. Police detectives conduct inquiries to solve crimes. A scientific inquiry is an investigation to solve science problems. It includes all the things you do and the thought process applied to help you arrive at a solution. Scientific investigations may start with an observation that leads to a research question. What is your research question in this investigation?

The research questions is, “What is in the box?”

Try doing the things you suggested. Write down what you do and what you observe. Include as much detail as you can.

Make sure students recognize the difference between what they do (tilt the box side to side) and what they observe (the object does not roll side to side).

4. Your hypothesis

Now you will use your observations to make a hypothesis about what is inside the box. Now answer the questions in section 4.

Students should discuss the observations they made and should come up with a hypothesis that fits the observations they have made so far.

Communicating Results

When conducting investigations, students are sometimes tempted to force the results to match their initial hypothesis. Students at this age often equate being wrong with inadequacy or failure. Use this time to explain to students that doing science is a process. Part of the process is truthfully reporting their experimental techniques and results. Emphasize that objectivity is the responsibility of all scientists.

5. Testing your hypothesis

A hypothesis is an educated guess that can be tested with an experiment. Think of one additional test you can do to help you figure out if your hypothesis is correct. The test should be something you have not done yet. Do the test and write down your results. Then do any other tests that will help you figure out the shape of your mystery object.

Students should work with their group members to come up with ideas for tests to confirm or deny their hypotheses. Make sure students write down all of their observations.

6. Stop and think

Observations

Students often do not realize the difference between observations and conclusions they draw based on observations. Stress that observations require the use of the five senses. An observation is something students can see, smell, touch, taste, or hear.

A correct hypothesis agrees with all of the observations. If even one observation goes against the hypothesis, then the hypothesis cannot be perfectly correct. Work through the questions in Part 6 of the investigation. You have to think about each of your observations and decide whether or not it supports your hypothesis.

Students determine whether their hypothesis was correct by going through all of their observations.

Once you have thought about all of your observations and you think you know the shape that is inside, you can open your box and see if you were correct. If you were wrong, think of another test you could have done to help you make additional observations.

Students find out if their hypotheses were correct. If students were incorrect, discuss additional tests they could have done to make more observations.

7. Exploring on your own

For homework, you will be making a mystery box using things you find around the house. It should contain one, two, or three objects. Then you will write down four tests that would allow someone to figure out what’s in your mystery box.

Investigation 1.2: Data and Answers

1. Setting up

2. Stop and think

  1. The sphere would roll easily in any direction. The cube would slide or tumble if the box were slanted. The cylinder would only roll in one direction. The cone would roll but would turn rather than roll in a straight line.
  2. Tilt the box gently to the left and right and feel if the object rolls or stays in place. Tilt the box gently forward and backward and feel if the object rolls or stays in place. Tilt the box at a much steeper angle left and right and feel/listen to determine if the object rolls or tumbles. Repeat with the box tilted forward and backward.

3. Conducting your inquiry

Table 1: Results of your inquiry

What you did

What you observed (evidence)

Tilt box gently left/right

Object does not move

Tilt box gently forward/backward

Object does not move

Tilt box at steep angle left/right

Object sounds and feels like it is tumbling

Tilt box at steep angle forward/backward

Object sounds and feels like it is tumbling

4. Your hypothesis

  1. I believe a cube is in the box.
  2. The object did not move when the angle was not steep. The object sounded and felt like it was tumbling when the box was tilted at a steep angle.
  3. I did not feel or hear the object move smoothly in the box as if it was rolling. I did not observe motion when the box was slightly tilted. I did not observe a difference in motion when the box was tilted left/right and forward/backward.

5. Testing your hypothesis

  1. Get the object into the center of the box. Slowly tilt the box to the right at a steeper and steeper angle until the object moves. Then make the box level again and see if the object stops moving.
  2. The object stopped moving when I stopped tilting the box.
  3. Shake the box up and down. Feel and listen to the object as it hits the top and bottom of the box. Try to figure out if the sound/feeling is the same when it hits the top and bottom. Observation: It feels/sounds like a solid side is hitting the top and bottom of the box. The sound is the same for the top and bottom.

6. Stop and think

  1. The object in the box is a cube.
  2. The object did not move when the box was tilted at a slight angle left/right or forward/back. The object sounded and felt like it was tumbling when the box was tilted at a steeper angle in either direction. If the box was tilted just enough to make it start to tumble, the object stopped moving once the box was returned to a horizontal position. It sounded and felt the same when I shook the box and the object hit the top and bottom of the box.
  3. Each observation supports my hypothesis.
  4. I was correct.

7. Exploring on your own

  1. Students answers will vary.

Explain

Class Time: 30 minutes

Assign the Lesson 1.2 Reading: Thinking Like a Scientist. Use the Lesson 1.2 Presentation Slides as a guide to presenting and discussing the content in the reading.

Addressing Misconceptions: Hypotheses, Theories, and Laws

As students’ investigative skills improve, they infer that science is a process which usually involves many revisions. Students realize that many hypotheses are initially incorrect. In these cases, the hypothesis must be revised and tested further. If the results of an investigation validate a hypothesis, students may think the hypothesis is “advanced to the next level” and becomes a theory. This is a common misconception. Explain to students that a hypothesis must be tested repeatedly by different scientists in order to be validated. Even if the hypothesis withstands the review of other scientists, there is no guarantee that it will become a theory.


Developing and Writing a Hypothesis

If students have been introduced to a hypothesis before this time, they may have defined it simply as an educated guess. A hypothesis provides an explanation for a set of observations that can include a variety of scientific concepts, principles, and theories. Students are expected to be able to develop a hypothesis as they design experiments. Some characteristics of a well-written hypothesis are given in the following statements.

  1. A hypothesis offers an explanation for observations.
  2. A hypothesis is based on prior experiences or knowledge, facts, research, or other observations.
  3. A hypothesis leads to or includes a testable prediction.

Share this example with students. Suppose a friend placed a glass of ice cubes on a shelf in a room. After several hours, your friend returned to the room and observed that the ice cubes had melted. How might you write a hypothesis about the observation?

The student may already know that ice is frozen water that changes state in certain temperature conditions. For example, water freezes at or below 32°F or 0°C. This prior knowledge coupled with the student’s observation may lead to a hypothesis. For example, “The temperature in the room exceeded the freezing point of water. Heat from the surroundings warmed the glass and caused the ice to melt.”

There are a number of different ways to write hypotheses. One method that many students tend to understand easily is the “If-Then” technique. The if- then format implies that a relationship exists between two variables such as temperature and the phase of matter. Apply the if-then method as your students practice writing hypotheses for each observation described below. Sample responses are shown in parentheses.

  • A daisy plant placed in direct sunlight grew taller than another daisy plant placed in the shade. (If a daisy plant needs sunlight to grow, then a plant placed in the sunlight will grow taller than a plant placed in the shade.)
  • California residents experienced more earthquakes than Texas residents in 2012. (If earthquakes occur more frequently near faults, then a person living in California will experience more earthquakes than a person living in Texas.

A theory explains how or why something occurs and is verified by known facts. However as new evidence is gathered, theories may also be modified or even discarded. Students often ask, “How long does it take for a theory to become a law?” Embedded in this question is the belief that a definite pattern exists between hypotheses, theories, and laws. A law states a scientific fact. A law is a precept in nature and a theory attempts to explain the precept. Use this graphic to help students understand the correct relationship between hypotheses, theories, and laws.
