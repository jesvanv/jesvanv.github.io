Teaching Lesson 1.1

Students should complete Chapter 1 Pretest and Chapter 1 Study Guide before starting the lesson cycle.

The Study Guide prepares students for chapter content by summarizing main points and vocabulary. Student should read the Study Guide aloud.

Engage

Class Time: 25 minutes for discussion

Sensory Stations

Materials needed: individually wrapped packages of flavored jelly beans, laminated copies of comic and activity pages from a newspaper, compact disc for recording distinct musical sounds, headphones, blindfolds, baby food jars with lids, coffee beans, whole garlic cloves, moth balls, vinegar, peppermint sticks or pieces, and plastic containers with the following materials added: gelatin, cotton balls, pinecones, peach, prickly pear, sandpaper.

Set up the sensory stations described below. At each station, students will rely on their senses to make observations. Explain that for safety reasons, scientists rarely use the sense of taste in the laboratory. (Mineralogists may use taste to identify certain minerals, such as halite). However, if you want to include taste, then purchase individually wrapped packages of flavored jelly beans and ask students to describe the different flavors they taste. Another option is to have students describe the taste of common foods as bitter, salty, sweet, or sour.

Sensory Station

Activity

Hearing

Create a CD with the sounds of 10 musical instruments. Have students wear headphones to listen to the sounds, and then identify each instrument.

Touch

Students wear a blindfold (or close their eyes) and identify unknown materials. Set up plastic containers with each of the following materials: gelatin, cotton balls, pinecones, peach, prickly pear, and sandpaper. Because the gelatin is messy, it should be the last material the students touch. Have wet wipes available for students to clean their hands afterwards.

Smell

Students wear a blindfold (or close their eyes) to identify items by their unique smell. Place various items—coffee beans, garlic cloves, moth balls, vinegar, peppermint—in lidded baby food jars. Demonstrate the wafting process as a safe method for smelling unknown objects. Have students replace the lid on each jar immediately after smelling each sample.

Sight

Obtain the “I Spy” activity from the comic pages of your local newspaper. If the picture is too small, enlarge it before laminating it. Have students locate the oddly placed items. A second option is the “What’s wrong with this picture?” activity. Have students observe differences between two images that at first glance appear the same.

VOCABULARY

qualitative observation – describes a quality such as shape, color, or size

quantitative observation – uses a measurement or number to describe something

inference – an opinion or judgment

Explore

Class Time: 50 minutes

Students will learn how to use the DataCollector and photogate equipment. By doing a series of inquiry activities, they must deduce the rules by which the DataCollector works from observing what happens as objects move in and out of the photogate. The investigation is important for learning to measure and work with time, but it is almost more important as a model for learning through experiment rather than being told what will happen. Since inquiry-based investigation is the foundation of the course, this investigation plays a critical role in establishing the protocol that will be followed in the subsequent investigations. The concepts of accuracy, resolution, and precision are introduced through time measurements made with photogates and with a stopwatch.

Setup Instructions

Materials
  1. Students work in groups of 3-5
  2. Become familiar with the features of the DataCollector before introducing it to students.
  • DataCollector
  • Photogate

Investigation 1.1: Measuring Time

A good part of the usefulness of science is knowing how to predict what will happen next. The word next implies the passage of time, and much of our study of science deals with the way things change over time. A ball rolls down a hill and its motion can be described by where it is at any time after it starts moving. Today’s investigation is about the measurement of time. Many experiments in physics will require us to measure time very precisely, often to one ten-thousandth of a second. A simple stopwatch will not usually be adequate. For accurate time measurements we will use a digital photogate DataCollector. Today’s investigation is about how this measuring tool works and understanding its limitations.

Reaction time and driving

The importance of reaction time is often mentioned when discussing automobile braking to avoid an accident. If a driver is in a situation in which he or she must quickly brake, a fast reaction time can help prevent a collision. Other factors such as the speed at which the car is moving, the effectiveness of the brakes, and the condition of the road are important in determining the distance required to bring the car to a stop.

A driver’s reaction time depends on a number of factors. Lack of sleep and the ingestion of alcohol or drugs both can substantially decrease reaction time. This is why driving when intoxicated or under the influence of drugs (even certain prescription and over the counter drugs) is so dangerous. What are some other factors that might influence a driver’s reaction time? Have students generate a list of ideas. Encourage students to design experiments to test their ideas; or, they may conduct research to learn about similar studies that have already been completed.

1. Stopwatch mode

You have already learned how the DataCollector is able to function in several modes, including in stopwatch mode. Turn on the DataCollector and put it in stopwatch mode.

Students used the DataCollector in stopwatch mode in Investigation 1.1. If necessary, lead a brief refresher (see the DataCollector Setup Video). Remind students that they can always confirm that they are in the correct mode by checking the status bar.

Take a few minutes to practice starting and stopping the stopwatch, and then resetting it to zero. Who can get the fastest time? Have each group member record his or her time after several tries. Then take the average time for each person.

Encourage students as they try to get the fastest time. Write the times on the board. Start by asking students if they can get a time shorter than one second. The fastest time may in the range of 0.1–0.2 second.

Where does your time go? Fit it in the list.

As each group calls out the time, add it to a list on the blackboard.

Imagine two runners who completed the 100-yard dash. One did the dash in 10.00 seconds, and the other took 10.01 seconds. Who wins?

The students should respond by saying the 10.00-second runner wins. Emphasize that greater speed means lower time, which is an inverse relationship.

Suppose we had the best stopwatch in the world. Could we tell the difference between the winner and the loser?

Prompt the students to look at the times you just put on the board, and conclude the answer is no. The difference is 0.01 second, and the best reaction time you will have been able to get will be at least seven times too large (0.07 s). Human reaction time is about 0.1 second, the signal travel time for a nerve from the brain to a muscle. The loss of precision is due to human reaction time.

2. Using the photogate

Connect one photogate to the Photogate A input of the DataCollector. This time you will use the DataCollector in CPO Timer Mode. Select this mode from the home screen. In Timer mode, there are four different functions—interval, frequency, period, and count. Select interval, by tapping the interval function button at the bottom of the screen. You will know you are in the correct mode when you see the photogate icon, and the word Interval in the status bar.

Confirm that students are in the proper mode.

3. Thinking about what you learned

When you attach only one photogate to the Photogate A port, the DataCollector records the time that photogate A’s beam was interrupted. The time measurement begins when the light beam is broken in photogate A, and stops when the light beam is unbroken again. This time is labeled tA. Take your finger and block the light beam. Describe what happens. What do you do to start and stop the clock now?

Have the students experiment with the light beam until they can tell you exactly what starts and stops the clock. A good answer is, “Blocking the beam starts the clock and unblocking the beam stops it.” An insufficient answer is, “Your finger does it.” Getting the entire answer often takes several prompts, such as, “Okay, what stops it?” You can keep up the contest for getting the smallest time interval. Flicking a finger or pencil point through the beam eventually produces a time of 0.0001 second. Again, use the board to keep track of the list of times.

If you block the light beam several times in a row, do the times add or do they start from zero each time? Figure out an experiment to test this and show me.

The point is to introduce the concept of hypothesis and testing the hypothesis by comparing it with experimental evidence. Most students say “add” without thinking about it. Students should see that flicking a finger through the beam repeatedly does not always increase the number. This observation demonstrates that the DataCollector does not add every time the light beam is blocked.

Have students answer question c. and then check their answer by using the DataCollector and observing the readout displayed on its screen. The answer to d. will vary, but in general using the stopwatch will give much shorter time intervals when attempting to obtain the shortest time as compared to the manually operated stopwatch. The photogate could be used to time the marble as it falls by placing an object, like a finger, in the light beam’s pathe to start the clock. When the object hits the floor, the object could be removed, which would stop the clock. This would be similar to using the stopwatch function, but insted of tapping the buttons on the screen, a person could use the photogate tomeasure the time the marble took to fall. Two photogates could also be used to measure the time the marble was in the air, one to start the timing, and one to stop the timing.

Investigation 1.1: Data and Answers

1. Stopwatch mode

Sample answers:

  1. The stopwatch measures time to the nearest hundredth of a second.
  2. Answers will vary based on each student’s data.
  3. The start/stop time represents reaction time.
  4. Each person in the class is unlikely to have the same average start/stop time because reaction time is unique to individuals and influenced by a number of factors.
  5. The start/stop times may be affected by a number of factors including the student’s technique, age, and ability to focus on the task.
  6. When starting and stopping the stopwatch repeatedly, the time may vary. However, with each try, it is possible to improve technique and to get more precise data.
  7. Yes. You could start where the marble is dropped and stop when it hits the floor.

2. Using the photogate

Answers will vary.

3. Thinking about what you learned

Sample answers:

  1. Blocking the beam starts the DataCollector. Unblocking the beam stops the DataCollector.
  2. The DataCollector starts at zero every time you break the beam, because the time does not keep getting longer when you block the beam several times in a row.
  3. Time is displayed to the nearest ten thousandth of a second.
  4. Answers will vary, but the increased resolution of the Timer provides more precise data.
  5. The DataCollector with the photogate would work because it is capable of measuring time intervals and it is not dependent on reaction time. Instead, the time recorded is based on the photogate sensing when the light beam is broken, which starts and stops the timer.

Explain

Class Time: 30 minutes

Assign the Lesson 1.1 Reading: Observing the World. Use the Lesson 1.1 Presentation Slides as a guide to presenting and discussing the content in the reading.

Guided Discussion: Distinguishing Between Observations and Inferences

Display a nature scene such as an image depicting the autumn season. Then ask students to share observations that would adequately describe the scenery. Create a two-column table. Label the first column Observations. Record the students’ observations in this column.

Explain that making an observation requires relying on senses to describe an object or event. The evidence supporting the observation is based on facts that can be easily evaluated. However, making an inference requires a person to consider past experiences or prior knowledge to logically explain observations.

Ask students to reflect once more on the observations they made about the autumn scenery. Ask, “How did you use those observations to infer the season represented in the image?” Use students’ answers to complete the second column (labeled Inferences) of the table. Each student observation should be accompanied by an inference. An example is shown below.

Observations

Inferences

Some of the tree branches have shed their leaves.

In the past, I have observed how some trees lose their leaves as the summer season shifts to autumn.

Leaves have fallen to the ground and are scattered along the roadside.

It must be autumn because my family and I often rake and bag fallen leaves in October and November.

I see a mixture of red, yellow, orange, brown, and green leaves.

It is autumn because I learned in science class that some species of plants contain pigments that become more visible during fall months.

Repeat this process with other images or objects to ensure that students are able to distinguish between observations and inferences. Extend the discussion by having students further classify observations as quantitative or qualitative.

The words qualitative and quantitative may be a bit confusing to students since they are spelled similarly and sound somewhat alike. Write both words and their Latin roots and meanings (qualis meaning “of what kind” and quantitus meaning “how much”) on the board. Draw students’ attention to the root of each word (quality and quantity) as you underline it. Explain that quality refers to characteristics such as color and shape. Quantity answers the question “how much” or “how many.”

Have students make qualitative and quantitative observations about objects in your classroom. Use the origins to phrase questions. For example, “Of what shape is this book? How much mass does this pencil have?”

Elaborate

Class Time: 20 minutes

Scientists use observation skills and make inferences

Discuss the significance of observation and inference to scientists and explain how scientists are sometimes able to answer questions by observation alone; but, at other times, they must make inferences. Read each of the following descriptions of tasks performed by scientists. Have students tell whether the task can be performed by observation alone or if an inference must also be made.

  • A meteorologist uses models and historical records of weather trends to predict the number of hurricanes expected to impact the US Gulf Coast.
  • A microbiologist views tiny organisms in a sample of pond water.
  • A nutritionist reads a label to determine the number of calories in a serving of potato chips.
  • An archaeologist locates an interesting artifact and hopes to identify the culture to which it is connected.
  • A geologist predicts that a specific region of the US will be affected by an earthquake.
  • A physicist measures the exact distance an object travels after it is launched.

Summarize important facts about observations and inferences. Use review statements, examples, or visuals to emphasize these points. Summary statements may include the following:

  1. Scientists use their senses to make observations about their surroundings.
  2. Scientists merge these observations with what they already know to make inferences.
  3. Making inferences is about reasoning and using logic to explain your observations or something you think is likely to occur.
